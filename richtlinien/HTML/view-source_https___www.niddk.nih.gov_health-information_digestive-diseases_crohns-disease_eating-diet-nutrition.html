<h1 itemprop="name">Eating, Diet, & Nutrition for Crohn’s Disease</h1>
<div class="health-detail-content">
<h2 id="eat">What should I eat if I have Crohn’s disease?</h2>
<p>If you have Crohn’s disease, you should choose <a href="/health-information/diet-nutrition">healthy foods and drinks</a>. Talk with your doctor about a healthy eating plan.</p>
<p>Crohn’s disease can affect nutrition in several ways. Crohn’s disease <a href="/health-information/digestive-diseases/crohns-disease/symptoms-causes#symptoms">symptoms</a> may cause some people to lose their appetite and eat less. <a class="dictionary-term" href="/Dictionary/I/inflammation">Inflammation</a> in the <a class="dictionary-term" href="/Dictionary/S/small-intestine">small intestine</a> may cause the body to absorb fewer nutrients. Certain <a href="/health-information/digestive-diseases/crohns-disease/treatment#medicines">medicines</a>&nbsp;and <a href="/health-information/digestive-diseases/crohns-disease/treatment#surgery">surgery</a>&nbsp;used to treat Crohn’s disease may also cause the body to absorb fewer nutrients.</p>
<p>Depending on your symptoms and the medicines you take, your doctor may recommend changes to what you eat and drink.<sup>10</sup> Your doctor may also recommend dietary supplements if your body doesn’t absorb enough nutrients. For safety reasons, talk with your doctor before using <a href="https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/WYNTK-Consumer/">dietary supplements</a>, such as vitamins, or any <a href="https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/complementary-alternative-or-integrative-health-whats-in-a-name">complementary or alternative</a> medicines or medical practices.</p>
<p>Getting the amount of nutrients that is best for you may help prevent <a href="/health-information/digestive-diseases/crohns-disease/definition-facts#complications">complications</a>, such as <a href="/Dictionary/M/malnutrition" class="dictionary-term">malnutrition</a>&nbsp;and problems with growth and development in children.</p>
<h3>Diet</h3>
<p>Researchers have not found that specific foods cause or worsen Crohn’s disease symptoms. However, studies suggest that consuming healthy foods and drinks may lower the risk of developing <a href="/Dictionary/I/inflammatory-bowel-disease" class="dictionary-term">inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)</a>.</p>
<p>Talk with your doctor about any foods that seem to be related to your symptoms. Your doctor may suggest keeping a food diary to help identify foods that seem to make your symptoms worse.</p>
<h3>Gut bacteria</h3>
<p>Some experts believe that the <a href="/Dictionary/M/microbiome" class="dictionary-term">microbiome</a>&nbsp;may play a role in Crohn’s disease. Changes in certain types of <a href="/Dictionary/B/bacteria" class="dictionary-term">bacteria</a>&nbsp;in the <a href="/Dictionary/D/digestive-system" class="dictionary-term">digestive tract </a>may lead to IBD, including Crohn’s disease.</p>
<p>Eating a variety of healthy foods can improve healthy bacteria in the gut. Your doctor may recommend eating more fruits and vegetables for Crohn’s disease.</p>
